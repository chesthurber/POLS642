Bell-Ringer

Let’s say I wanted to get more precise results from my Gapminder analysis of GDP and Life Expectancy. A friend says “Why don’t you move from country-year to country-month units of analysis? Then you will have 12x the sample size, reducing your standard errors!”

Is there anything wrong with this?

Types of Datasets

1) Cross-sectional data: single observations from each unit (survey responses from a single wave)

2) Time series data: multiple observations over time from a single unit (daily stock prices)

3) Panel data: multiple observations over time from multiple unites (aka Time Series Cross-Sectional, TSCS)

Strengths and Weaknesses

  • allows you to get more observations from your data (maybe)

  • track within group variation

  • But data from within groups will be highly correlated

  • FEs was one approach to dealing with this

Fixed Effects

What Do We Do?The Logic of Fixed Effects

  • If you have grouped data…

  • Those groups are likely correlated with confounding variables

  • By controlling for the group (as a categorical variable), you control for all potentially confounding variables that are correlated with the group

Different Intercepts, Same Slope

More Advanced Choices

  • Moving Average: not just last year’s DV, but the average over a preceding period of time

  • ARMA: lagged DV and moving average terms

  • ARIMA: similar to deltas model described above

  • multiple levels of lags and moving average processes…

This is hard!

  • so many choices to make: fixed effects? ARMA? ARIMA? ARIMAX? SARIMAX?

  • best practice is to find an article modeling your DV or something similar, and follow their approach

BTSCS Data

When the DV is Binary

  • rather than thinking about a lagged DV, we might think about how many time increments have passed since the last occurrence of the DV

  • We will call these “spells” or “time spells”

  • it used to be that we would compute complex functions called “splines”

  • now we will just throw time spells into our model as controls, with squared and cubic polynomial terms

Conclusion

  • panel data offers lots of opportunities for examining more fine-grained variation

  • studying long-term changes in continuous variables over time is hard

  • with binary DVs, it’s a lot easier

  • pay attention to and copy practices you see in the literature
